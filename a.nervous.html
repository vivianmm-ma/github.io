Nervous System Basics

The Body's Command Center

To understand dysfunction, we must first understand function. The human nervous system is the body's command center, governing all thought, movement, and sensation. It is broadly divided into two main parts that are in constant communication.

CNS

Comprised of the brain and spinal cord, the CNS is the primary processing center where information is interpreted and decisions are made.

PNS

This consists of all the nerves that branch out from the CNS to the rest of the body, relaying information back and forth like a complex wiring network.

The Autonomic Nervous System (ANS)

A crucial subdivision of the PNS is the Autonomic Nervous System, which operates unconsciously to regulate bodily functions. Trauma disrupts the delicate balance between its two primary branches:

Sympathetic

(The "Gas Pedal" / Fight-or-Flight)

When a threat is perceived, this system floods the body with adrenaline and cortisol. It increases heart rate, sharpens senses, and diverts blood to the muscles, preparing the body for immediate, intense physical action.

Parasympathetic

(The "Brake Pedal" / Rest-and-Digest)

This system calms the body after the danger has passed. It lowers heart rate, promotes digestion, and facilitates cellular repair and healing. It's the state required for true rest and social connection.

A healthy nervous system can flexibly switch between these states. Trauma can lock the system into a state of chronic sympathetic activation.

The Brain Under Threat: Amygdala vs. Prefrontal Cortex

When your Sympathetic nervous system is activated, a critical shift happens in your brain. Resources are diverted from your thoughtful, rational brain to your primal, survival brain.

Amygdala (Smoke Detector)

This is your brain's threat-detection center. It operates on pure emotion and instinct, asking only one question: "Am I safe?" When it senses danger, it triggers the fight-or-flight response instantly.

Prefrontal Cortex (Watchtower)

This is the hub of higher-level thinking: logic, planning, and emotional regulation. It can assess a situation rationally and calm the amygdala down if a threat is not real.

During a stress response, the amygdala "hijacks" the brain. Blood flow and energy are pulled away from the Prefrontal Cortex. This is why it's so hard to "think straight" when anxious or angry. You are not operating at your full cognitive capacity because your brain is prioritizing survival over analysis.

The Conductor of Calm: The Vagus Nerve

The Vagus Nerve is the main component of the Parasympathetic ("Brake Pedal") system. Think of it as the superhighway connecting your brain to your major organs. The "tone" or health of your vagus nerve determines how quickly and effectively your body can return to a state of calm after a stressor.

A poorly functioning vagus nerve (low vagal tone) means the body struggles to apply the brakes, leaving you stuck in a state of high alert. This can manifest in symptoms like digestive issues, a persistent lump in the throat (globus sensation), and systemic inflammation.

An Unsung Hero: The Lymphatic System

Often overlooked, the lymphatic system is the body's waste-clearance network. Unlike the circulatory system which has the heart, the lymphatic system relies on deep breathing and muscle movement to function.

Chronic muscle tension and shallow, stress-based breathing—hallmarks of a dysregulated nervous system—can physically impede lymphatic flow. This leads to a buildup of inflammatory byproducts and toxins in the tissues, contributing to brain fog, fatigue, pain, and a compromised immune response.
